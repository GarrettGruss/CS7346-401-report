\section{Cost Considerations}
\label{sec:cost}

Cost for this platform is driven mainly by the mix of devices deployed in each household, the amount of activity generated by those devices, and how heavily each user interacts with the system. In practice, this means a home with a single lock and light has a very different cost profile than a home with multiple cameras, longer retention settings, and more frequent playback. To estimate this behavior at a reasonable scale, a Monte Carlo simulation was used with AWS pricing inputs drawn from the pricing constants in Table~\ref{tab:pricing-constants}.

\subsection{Simulation Methodology}

The Monte Carlo simulation was developed over a synthetic population of 1,000 households. Each household was assigned independent random draws for usage intensity, number of non-camera devices, camera adoption, number of cameras, video retention period, daily API call rate, IoT telemetry rate, camera recording time, camera viewing time, number of configured automation rules, and notification opt-in behavior. Usage intensity was modeled with a clipped normal distribution, while other variables used Bernoulli, discrete uniform, or weighted categorical distributions as appropriate for the scenario. All downstream cost quantities were then derived algebraically using published AWS pricing for the us-east-1 region. No free-tier credits were applied except for Amazon Cognito, since 1,000 monthly active users remain fully within its free tier.

Table~\ref{tab:sim-inputs} summarizes the input variables used in the simulation. The AWS pricing constants applied to each service are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix-pricing} (Table~\ref{tab:pricing-constants}).

The simulation produced a mean total cost of \$23.12 per household per month across the 1,000-household population, which corresponds to approximately \$23,100 per month, or \$277,400 per year, at this scale. Costs break down into three broad categories: storage at roughly 62\% of total spend, compute at roughly 32\%, and network egress at roughly 6\%.

Storage is the dominant cost category primarily because of Amazon Kinesis Video Streams. Camera-equipped households, which account for 45\% of the simulated population, accumulate more than 400 GB of video per camera each month at 1080p before retention is considered. As a result, KVS retention becomes the single largest line item in the model, and the fleet-wide average becomes highly sensitive to camera adoption rate, average camera count, and default retention policy.

Within compute, AWS IoT Core and AWS Lambda are the largest contributors. IoT Core costs grow with both device-connection minutes and message volume, while Lambda costs grow with API-driven processing, IoT-triggered events, and automation rule evaluation. DynamoDB on-demand capacity adds further cost because API activity and a portion of incoming device traffic both update persistent state.

Network egress remains comparatively small. Static frontend content benefits from CloudFront caching, so repeated origin fetches are limited. The largest egress component is S3 retrieval for archived camera playback, but this remains moderate in the model because only about 10\% of stored video is assumed to be retrieved during a typical billing period.

\subsection{Cost Scaling with Usage}

LightsPlease's cost structure scales differently across three layers: device activity, user-facing application activity, and media usage. IoT Core messaging, device indexing, and associated Lambda processing scale mainly with the number of installed modules. This creates a relatively predictable monthly floor per household. Web-side costs including API Gateway, CloudFront, DynamoDB reads, and user-triggered Lambda invocations scale more directly with user engagement, so households that check status more often or configure more automations generate higher monthly cost. Media costs are the most volatile layer. KVS ingest, retention, and S3 archival all scale with camera count, recording hours, and retention duration, so camera-heavy households can cost an order of magnitude more than households without cameras.

Some platform-level costs also amortize more favorably as the fleet grows. AWS WAF, CloudWatch alarms, and similar shared operational costs are spread across the installed base rather than scaling linearly per household. Amazon Cognito also remains free through 50,000 monthly active users, so identity cost does not become material until the platform grows well beyond the initial projection of several thousand households.

\subsection{Cost, Performance, and Reliability Tradeoffs}

DynamoDB was modeled in on-demand mode as an early-stage tradeoff that reduces operational tuning and avoids the risk of underprovisioning during uncertain growth. This is more expensive per request than provisioned capacity, but it is a reasonable choice while traffic remains unpredictable. As the fleet grows and usage patterns stabilize, migrating high-traffic tables to provisioned capacity with auto-scaling would likely reduce cost without sacrificing availability.

Kinesis Video Streams was selected over a direct-to-S3 camera pipeline because it supports low-latency live streaming and provides a managed ingestion path for camera traffic. That choice is materially more expensive than an archival-only design, but it directly supports one of the platform's core use cases. A likely optimization would be to keep only recent footage in KVS for live access and move older video into lower-cost S3 archival tiers, accepting slightly higher retrieval latency for older recordings.

AWS Lambda was chosen for backend processing because its pay-as-you-go model fits a workload that is bursty and event-driven rather than steady throughout the day. That keeps the cost floor low during quiet periods and avoids maintaining always-on application servers during early growth. The trade-off is that Lambda can become less cost-efficient at much higher sustained invocation volume, at which point a container-based platform such as Amazon ECS Fargate may become more economical for selected workloads.
