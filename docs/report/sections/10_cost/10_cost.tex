\section{Cost Considerations}

Cost modeling for a consumer IoT platform is more complicated than for a conventional web application because usage is driven by two largely independent dimensions: the behavior of end users interacting with the frontend, and the autonomous behavior of connected devices generating telemetry regardless of whether the user is active. Costs therefore have a baseline component tied to the number of registered devices and a variable component tied to user engagement. The platform must also support a wide range of household configurations — from a single smart lock to a full suite of cameras, thermostats, and sensors — which means per-user costs can vary substantially within the same pricing tier.

\subsection{Simulation Methodology}

To produce defensible cost estimates, a Monte Carlo simulation was developed over a synthetic population of 1,000 households. Each household was assigned independent random draws for the following variables: a usage intensity scalar sampled from a clipped normal distribution (mean 0.5, standard deviation 0.2, bounded to the interval [0.05, 1.0]); a device count sampled uniformly from one to twelve non-camera modules; a camera adoption indicator (Bernoulli, 45\% probability) that, when positive, assigned one to four cameras to the household; a video retention policy sampled from a weighted discrete distribution favoring fourteen-day retention; a daily API call rate ranging from 15 to 250 calls linearly scaled by usage intensity; an IoT telemetry rate of 50 to 600 messages per device per day; a number of configured automation rules from zero to fifteen; and a notification opt-in flag (Bernoulli, 70\% probability). All downstream cost quantities were derived algebraically from these inputs using published AWS pricing for the us-east-1 region. No free-tier credits were applied, with the exception of Amazon Cognito, for which 1,000 monthly active users fall entirely within the 50,000-user free tier and therefore contribute no direct cost.

The simulation was executed using NumPy's vectorized random generation, which produces all 1,000 user arrays simultaneously without iteration. Each AWS service cost was computed as a per-user array, summed or averaged over the population to produce fleet-level and per-user-mean estimates. The complete simulation dataset and a visual breakdown of results are included as supplementary artifacts.

\subsection{Major Cost Drivers}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{sections/10_cost/cost_distribution.png}
  \caption{Monte Carlo cost distribution across 1,000 simulated households. Left: cost share by high-level category. Right: mean monthly cost per AWS service, colored by category.}
  \label{fig:cost-distribution}
\end{figure}

The simulation produced a mean total cost of \$23.12 per household per month across the 1,000-user population, equivalent to a fleet-wide cost of approximately \$23,100 per month, or \$277,400 per year, at this scale. Costs decompose into three high-level categories: storage at approximately 62\% of total spend, compute at approximately 32\%, and network egress at approximately 6\%.

Storage dominates primarily because of Amazon Kinesis Video Streams. Camera-equipped households — 45\% of the simulated population — incur substantial video retention costs: a single 1080p camera recording ten hours per day at roughly 1.35 GB per hour accumulates over 400 GB per month before accounting for the Kinesis retention window. At the simulated video retention distribution (mean approximately seventeen days), KVS storage alone accounts for the majority of the storage category. This sensitivity is structurally important: a platform decision to allow longer default retention periods or to encourage higher camera counts has a direct and non-linear effect on per-user cost. Households without cameras spend a small fraction of what camera-equipped households spend, which means the fleet average is strongly influenced by camera adoption rate.

Within the compute category, the two largest contributors are AWS IoT Core and AWS Lambda. IoT Core charges accrue from both message volume and device-connection minutes: a household with six devices connected continuously over a thirty-day month contributes over 260,000 device-connection minutes, plus tens of thousands of telemetry messages depending on device activity level. Lambda costs scale with the union of API-driven invocations, IoT-triggered processing (modeled at 20\% of incoming telemetry messages), and automation rule evaluations. At median usage intensity, a household generates on the order of 3,000 to 6,000 Lambda invocations per month across these three sources. DynamoDB on-demand capacity also contributes meaningfully to compute spend: every API call triggers approximately three read request units and one write unit, and 10\% of IoT messages are assumed to update persistent device state.

Network egress costs are relatively modest because the primary data transfer path — static frontend assets — is served from S3 through CloudFront, which caches aggressively and reduces repeated origin fetches. The dominant network cost component is S3 transfer for camera archival playback: approximately 10\% of archived video is assumed to be retrieved by users over the billing period, which generates meaningful egress charges for camera-heavy households. CloudFront per-request fees are small in absolute terms given that household-level API call volumes remain well below the million-request pricing threshold.

\subsection{Cost Scaling with Usage}

The platform's cost structure scales differently across its three layers. Device-side costs — IoT Core messages, IoT Device Management fleet indexing, and the associated Lambda processing — scale primarily with the number of registered devices per household and are largely insensitive to whether the user is actively engaging with the application. These costs represent a fixed monthly floor per household that grows predictably as households add modules. Web-side costs — API Gateway, CloudFront, DynamoDB reads, and user-triggered Lambda invocations — scale with user engagement intensity and are more variable across the population; heavy users who check the application frequently or configure many automations spend measurably more per month than passive users with the same device count. Media costs — KVS ingest, KVS retention, and S3 archival — scale with camera count and recording hours and are by far the most volatile dimension; a household moving from one to four cameras with extended retention can increase its monthly cost by an order of magnitude relative to a non-camera household.

At larger fleet sizes, several fixed and semi-fixed costs amortize favorably. AWS WAF incurs a flat charge per web ACL and per managed rule group regardless of request volume; this overhead becomes negligible per user as the fleet grows. CloudWatch metrics and alarm charges are similarly fixed at the platform level and spread across all households. Amazon Cognito remains free through 50,000 monthly active users, meaning Cognito costs do not appear until the platform reaches a scale well beyond the initial three-year projection of thousands of units.

\subsection{Cost, Performance, and Reliability Tradeoffs}

Several architectural decisions in this design involve meaningful cost tradeoffs against performance or reliability objectives. The choice to use DynamoDB in on-demand capacity mode rather than provisioned capacity reflects an early-stage tradeoff: on-demand pricing carries a higher per-request unit cost than provisioned capacity but eliminates the risk of underprovisioning during unpredictable growth and avoids paying for idle capacity during low-traffic periods. As the fleet grows and traffic patterns stabilize, migrating high-traffic tables to provisioned capacity with auto-scaling would reduce DynamoDB spend significantly without sacrificing availability.

Kinesis Video Streams was selected over a direct-to-S3 camera pipeline specifically because it supports low-latency live streaming and provides a managed ingestion layer that removes the need for a custom media server. The cost of this capability is meaningfully higher than a deferred S3-only archival model, but it directly enables the live camera monitoring use case that is central to the product. A cost-reduction path available at greater scale would be to implement tiered storage: retain only the most recent 24 hours in KVS for live access and flush older footage to S3 Glacier Instant Retrieval for long-term archival at dramatically lower storage rates, at the cost of a slightly higher retrieval latency for older recordings.

AWS Step Functions adds a modest but non-negligible cost for automation workflows because every state transition in a workflow incurs a charge. For simple single-step automations, Step Functions is more expensive than invoking a Lambda function directly, but the expressiveness and auditability it provides for multi-step conditional sequences justifies the overhead for complex rules. Platforms with primarily simple trigger-action automations could reduce spend by routing those workflows through a lightweight Lambda dispatcher rather than Step Functions, reserving the orchestration service for sequences that genuinely require coordinated multi-step execution.

Finally, AWS Lambda's serverless billing model provides a natural cost floor during periods of low activity — the platform incurs no compute charges when no requests are being processed — which is particularly favorable for a consumer home automation product where usage is concentrated in morning and evening windows rather than distributed uniformly across the day. However, at very high invocation rates, the per-GB-second compute cost of Lambda can exceed the equivalent cost of a continuously running container on Amazon ECS Fargate. This crossover point is not reached at the 1,000-household scale modeled here, but it is a transition that the platform operator should monitor as the fleet scales into the tens of thousands of households.
